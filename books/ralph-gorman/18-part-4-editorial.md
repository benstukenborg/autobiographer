Ralph's career after United Technology split into two parallel tracks, and the combination was unusual enough that it bears explaining.

He became a physics professor at Bronx Community College, teaching twelve hours a week — thirty weeks a year, with twenty-two weeks off. Community colleges in the 1960s and 1970s were the workhorses of American higher education, open to anyone, affordable to everyone, staffed by professors who were often more devoted to teaching than to research. The pay was poor. When Ralph started, engineers were making twenty thousand dollars a year; teachers were making six thousand. But the schedule was extraordinary. Ralph took a sabbatical every three years — at no pay, or at half pay — because he could afford to. He could afford to because of his other career.

He became a semiconductor consultant, working one or two days a week for companies that needed problems solved. The semiconductor industry in the 1960s and 1970s was still young enough that a man who had spent six years learning every step of chip manufacturing — from crystal growth to packaging — was a rare and valuable thing. Most engineers had become specialists. Ralph had an overall view. When a production line went wrong, he could look at a chip and narrow the problem to a specific step, because he had done every step himself.

The teaching gave him time — time to think, to travel, to raise his children, to take his daughter to the beach every day all summer. The consulting gave him money and intellectual challenge. Together, they let him build a life that a dropout machinist from the Bronx was never supposed to have.
