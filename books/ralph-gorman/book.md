# Ralph Gorman: A Collection of Memoirs


---

## A Note on This Book

This book began as a series of conversations. Over the course of fifteen interviews conducted during his ninety-second year, Ralph Gorman told the stories of his life — his childhood in the Bronx, his years as a machinist and taxi driver, his transformation from a high school dropout into a physicist and engineer, the farm in Connecticut where he built a second life, and the people who shaped him along the way.

What you are about to read is not a biography in the traditional sense. It is a collection of memoirs, told in Ralph's own voice and drawn from his own words. The vast majority of this book — roughly ninety percent — is Ralph speaking in the first person. He is the storyteller. His stories are his.

The remaining ten percent is editorial narration: brief introductions to each section, observations that connect one memoir to the next, and historical context that Ralph himself does not provide. These passages are written in the third person and are set apart from Ralph's voice. Their purpose is to orient you, the reader — to provide dates, explain circumstances, and occasionally point out patterns that become visible only when you see the whole of a life at once. They are not interpretations. They do not presume to know what Ralph felt or thought. They are scaffolding, and the building is his.

Ralph's speaking style will be immediately recognizable: direct, warm, prone to digression, laced with dry humor, and relentlessly honest. He does not romanticize his failures or exaggerate his successes. He tells you what happened. When he repeats a phrase — "if it's something you wanna do and you have the wherewithal to do it, you can do it" — it is because he means it, and because it has proven true in his life more than once.

He was ninety-two when these stories were recorded. He is ninety-three now. The stories are his legacy.

# Part I: Where I Come From

========================================================================


## Editorial Frame


In the early twentieth century, Sephardic Jews from the Ottoman Empire began arriving in New York in significant numbers, settling in tight-knit neighborhoods where they could speak Ladino and cook the dishes they had carried from Constantinople, Salonika, and the ports of the Mediterranean. They were a minority within a minority — distinct from the Ashkenazi Jews who dominated Jewish life in the city, with their own temples, their own food shops, their own accent in every language they spoke. Most settled in the lower reaches of Manhattan at first, but by the 1920s and 1930s, communities had taken root in the Bronx.

Ralph Gorman was born into one of these communities in 1932. He was the youngest of five children — fifteen to twenty-one years younger than his siblings, an afterthought born to parents in their mid-forties. They lived in a two-bedroom apartment between 167th and 168th Streets with eight people: his parents in one room, his three sisters and brother in the other, his grandmother in the living room. A toddler, Ralph would climb over his sleeping sisters to get into bed at night.

His mother, Sophie Penso, spoke six languages and could sing in all of them. His father was abusive and uneducated — a low-level factory worker who worked nights while his wife played cards until two or three in the morning. Ralph was, by his own description, raised with benign neglect — a street kid who did what he wanted. He left school at fourteen. At twelve, when his father went to hit him, he said, "If you touch me, I'll kill you." His father backed off. That was the end of it.

No one in the neighborhood had gone to college. No one was a doctor, a lawyer, a plumber, or a carpenter. Everyone was a factory worker. The children played marbles and punchball in the streets, a hundred kids on a single block, the seasons of their games changing by some collective instinct no one could explain.

What follows are Ralph's memories of that world: his mother's languages, and the four siblings who fled the household before he was old enough to know them well.

---


## My Mother's Languages

My mother's name was Sophie Penso. Both my parents were born in Turkey. It was called Constantinople when they came, but now it's Istanbul. They were Sephardic Jews. And Sephardic Jews immigrated all over, mostly the Mediterranean. So I would have relatives in Italy, France, Spain, Morocco. My relatives were spread all over the Middle East and all over the Mediterranean.

Sephardic Jews speak Spanish as their native language. And they also speak the language of whatever country they settled in. So my mother had relatives in Morocco, Spain, Greece, Italy, France. She spoke six languages fluently. She could actually sing in all of them. Arabic because we had relatives in Morocco. Spanish because all Sephardics speak Spanish. Greek. French. English was her worst language.

And when she was speaking in one of those tongues, you would think she was native born to that culture. That's how good she was.

We used to have parties at the house, and my mother would sing and dance. She would sing in Greek, she would sing in French, she would sing in Spanish, and she would dance to the music of those three cultures. She could speak Arabic, so she would sing in Arabic too. She had a fabulous voice, and she could really dance. And everybody would be clapping and cheering her on. My father liked having company even though he was abusive. So we had a lot of parties.

In terms of our cultural background, we weren't religious, but we ate mainly Sephardic cooking. And Sephardic cooking is based on the country you settled in. Some of the recipes come from way back in Sephardic history, but a lot were modified by living in a different country. The Sephardics in Italy would make the same dish my mother made, but with slightly different ingredients because they were in a different country. A somewhat different flavor.

I grew up in a Sephardic neighborhood in the Bronx — between 167th and 168th Streets. Lower-middle-class. There were no professionals that I knew of. No doctors, lawyers, engineers, accountants. No tradespeople — no plumbers, no electricians, no carpenters. Everybody was a factory worker. Unskilled. A lot of people worked in the garment center — pushing a cart, pressing blouses. Pretty much unskilled labor throughout the whole neighborhood.

But it was a neighborhood where you never had to worry about having company because there was always somebody you could befriend. Every apartment building had ten or twenty kids in it. So you'd have forty buildings on the block, a hundred kids. And one of the things I never understood — even today, I think about it — is how the seasons would change. During the summer, all of a sudden, the whole street would be playing marbles. Fifty kids playing marbles on the whole block. And that might last two weeks. Then marbles were over, and it's punchball. Then stickball. Then street hockey — either you were wearing skates or just running with a hockey stick and a puck. Nobody decided when the season changed. It just automatically started. Literally a hundred kids doing whatever sport was in.

We had a separate temple from the European Jews — a very, very small temple. Out of my fifteen close friends, I would say at least five or six were Sephardic Jews. The neighborhood had two stores that sold Sephardic food. One was a tiny little store that sold mainly staples — beans, pastries like baklava. The other store sold knishes — Sephardic knishes, which are totally different. You could go there for ten cents and buy a Turkish knish on the way home from school. That was spectacular.

We weren't religious, but we observed some of the holidays. We didn't do much — we just ate the foods associated with that holiday. On Passover, we'd have a dish that was like fried matzah, but totally different than matzah brei because it was based on a different recipe. My sisters and my brother all learned how to cook Sephardic food a little better than I did because they grew up for a longer period of time with my parents. I know how to cook a little bit of it, but not like they could.

As I grew up and went to school, I found I had a good inclination for languages. I picked that up from my mother, I think. I spoke Spanish quite well. I was in Italy for a month and learned enough Italian to make myself understood everywhere — the train station, shopping, restaurants, the bus terminal. Nobody in the Italian neighborhood spoke English, so I had to learn. And in college, I took advanced Russian. I was reading the Russian newspaper, Novoye Slovo. I was pretty good. I was going to be a scientific translator, actually, but I realized it was hard to make a living doing that.

I got a job translating four or five pages of a Russian scientific article in my last semester. They were paying me two or three hundred dollars a page. I thought, wow, that's a nice way to do it. But I had to look up every single word because it was a wholly different vocabulary from reading a newspaper. It took me over a month to translate four pages. So I realized I couldn't be a scientific translator unless I threw myself into it head over heels. And by that point, there were other things pulling me in a different direction.

But my mother gave me that. The languages. The ear for it. The confidence that you could walk into a country and learn enough to get by.


---

Ralph's mother gave him languages and music and the confidence to walk into any country and make himself understood. His father gave him something else: the knowledge, absorbed before he could name it, that some houses are not safe, and that the people who grow up in them will spend the rest of their lives either escaping or recovering. All five of his father's children left school early. All five ended up in some kind of institution for wayward children. What Ralph knows of his siblings, he learned mostly from a distance — the youngest watching the oldest from across a gap of fifteen to twenty-one years.

---


## The Older Ones

I had a brother and three sisters, all deceased now. They were much older than I was — my brother was twenty-one years older, and my sisters were fifteen, sixteen, and seventeen years older. My parents were essentially finished having children when they had those four right in a row, and then I came along when they were in their mid-forties. I was the afterthought.

We all grew up in the same house, but we didn't really grow up together. By the time I was old enough to know what was going on, most of them were already married or gone or trying to get gone. That house — with my father yelling and screaming and throwing things — nobody wanted to stay in it longer than they had to. I think all of us were in some kind of institution at one point. Homes for children who didn't have any supervision. Wayward children, they called us. So we had that in common, at least.

My brother was very smart, but he stopped school after the eighth grade. During the war — World War II — he wasn't drafted because he was married and had a child. He was an able-bodied person, and they were looking for able-bodied men, so one of our relatives said, why don't you drive a truck for my company, and when the war is over, I'll give you a better opportunity to rise. So my brother said yes. He drove a truck cross-country for a company called King Cone. They made ice cream cones in a big factory — sugar cones and other types — and had customers all over the country, and he would deliver these big cans of cones. He did that for most of the war. And when the war ended, they fired him. This is one of our cousins. So much for that.

He was very disappointed. He bounced around. He wound up driving a provision truck, selling pastrami and corned beef and tongue and frankfurters to kosher delis. And he was living with his wife, a beautiful, beautiful woman who came from a very loving family. But my brother had a very bad childhood, and so he was very mean-spirited. Not violent — he would never think of raising his hands — but very angry. Anything would set him off, and he'd be angry and he wouldn't speak to you. He would do that to his wife, and she couldn't take it because she was a loving person from a loving family. He actually drove her to drink. And I mean that literally. She actually became, for a while, an alcoholic because of him.

He mellowed out eventually. He retired. His son didn't go to school, got divorced — his wife just walked out and left him with the two children, which was heartbreaking for my brother. I would say he had a tough life.

But I liked my brother. I always wanted to be with him. I would make every effort — he lived in Brooklyn — to go see him. And every once in a while, he'd be mad at me, and I could never figure out why. He wouldn't talk to me for weeks on end. I would call him and he'd say, I don't wanna talk to you. You did that. I don't know what I did. I couldn't remember. But I would apologize and say, listen, whatever I did, I'm sorry. No. It's too late now.

So what I would do is I would buy a case of Turkish whiskey. I'd call him up and say, listen, I have to come and see you. I have something for you. I want you to have it. I'd drive out to Brooklyn, give him the case of Turkish whiskey, and he would be in heaven because he loved it.

My sister Esther was the oldest of the girls — eighteen or nineteen years older than me. She was like a little bird. Always frightened. My father was always yelling and screaming and throwing things, and she would be in tears, hiding. It was a sad commentary on that house.

She had to escape, and she did the only way she knew how — she married young. Married when she was nineteen. Beautiful girl. And the man she chose was a prizefighter who I think was a little punch-drunk, actually, because he had a temper that was unbelievable. But he would never take it out on Esther. If she started crying, he would melt. Everyone else, though — he was frightening. If he threatened you, it was like assault with a deadly weapon. He could break a two-inch wooden hanger with two fingers. I mean, he had biceps that were bigger than my leg.

She had a baby right away, a year or so after they got married, and then she had twins. So now she had three small children and couldn't cope with her own life, let alone theirs. And he was working all the time, day and night. So I would babysit for her, come up and keep her company because she was lonely and she was frightened. Her marriage, I would say, was not made in heaven, but at least they stuck it out together for a long time. Her kids didn't go to school. They wound up marrying early too. And then they moved to Florida, and he died about six months after they got there. She stayed. Her children eventually moved down too. She expired in Florida. She was in her late nineties.

My sister Sally was seventeen years older than me — a little on the tall side for a woman, about five foot seven, very, very attractive. She also couldn't wait to escape our household. There was a guy who lived in the building, Joey Epstein, and he was always chasing after her. And she became a cosmetologist. She worked in Macy's in the cosmetics department, selling creams and lipsticks and rouges and eye shadow and face creams and whatever. She was very good at it, and she believed in it because she did it every night. Her face would be covered in cream. Her lips would have this. Her eyes would have that. So she was a talented cosmetologist, actually.

She did that all during the war, and when Joey came out of the army they got married and had two boys, one after the other. Joey was a truck driver, then he gave that up and became a salesman in the garment center, traveling around the country selling dresses. Very aggressive salesman. Then they moved to Florida, and he was at loose ends. Didn't know what to do. My sister was happy being alone. Happy. But she didn't like him being home, so she tried to get him a job as a cashier. He couldn't do it. He wasn't a very smart guy at all. No education and no brains. He was aggressively stupid. And so that was their life.

Now, here's the thing about Sally. I visited my sister Louise in Florida quite often. When I was down there, I would always see Sally too — we'd go to a restaurant. But in twenty-five years of traveling to Florida, she never invited me to her house once. I never saw her house. Not once. But she would come to Connecticut with her husband and spend two weeks here like it's a resort. Every year. Two weeks. And I never said anything. But I never went to her house once in twenty-five years.

Then there was Louise. My sister Louise was sixteen years older than me — the closest to me in age, and the closest to me in every other way. We had a very, very long, close relationship right up until the day she died.

She lived at home while I was still there. My other sisters and my brother were already out, and she was the one still living at home. So we kind of comforted each other. And she was the only one out of five children who went to high school. She took a lot of abuse for that, because my father would always call her "the school teacher" in an abusive way. But my sister was a dynamo. I mean, get out of the way. She was like a locomotive. She wanted to do something, she did it. She wanted something, she made sure she got it, either by hard work or whatever she had to do.

During World War II, she joined the Women's Auxiliary Corps, and she used to write me letters. I would write her back. So we had a long correspondence all during the war, four years. I always answered her letters, even though my English grammar was awful. I never went to high school, so I didn't really have good writing skills. But I wrote her back every time.

When she came out of the army, she married a very nice guy from New London, Connecticut — Harold. Very docile, easygoing, very family-oriented. The opposite of my sister in every way. Then there was some kind of scandal. He was selling shoes in Connecticut, and there was some money missing or something of that nature, and he got fired. So now he had no job and a bad reputation in a small town. They moved back to the Bronx and lived very close to where I was.

I was about eighteen or nineteen. I was a machinist. I had a car. So I let Harold use my car, and he became an over-the-road salesperson. He didn't do that well, and in a year or two my car was gone — ran into the ground. But we still maintained a good relationship.

Then I went back to school, and I got a degree. And my sister went back to school too. She got a master's degree, and I was actually helping her — tutoring her in her science and math courses. And then she became a school teacher. Which I thought was really interesting, given what my father used to call her.

They moved to Westchester, bought a house, had three children who I still see. In fact, just this weekend, my nephew was here with his two small children. I think they had a very good life. And the interesting thing about my nephew — his mother, my niece, is a statuesque beauty. She married a guy who just kept on eating and eating and eating until he was in a wheelchair. He couldn't move. He had to be wheeled around. He died at a very early age because of his weight. But that's the next generation.

After a while, all my sisters moved to Florida. My brother stayed in Brooklyn. The same house that five people had grown up trying to escape — it produced a truck driver, a frightened girl who married a prizefighter, a cosmetologist, and a schoolteacher. And me. Same house, same parents, five completely different lives.

The family gatherings we had in our old age, here at the farm in Connecticut, were very nice and very friendly. My daughter got married here, right down below. My sisters all came. I think my brother came. My nephews came. It was a big wedding. And now my granddaughter is getting married in exactly the same spot. A déjà vu.

# Part II: The Years of Drift

========================================================================


## Editorial Frame


In the late 1940s and early 1950s, New York was full of young men like Ralph Gorman — high school dropouts with no particular skills, no particular plans, and no particular reason to believe their lives would amount to much. The war was over. The economy was booming for people with educations and connections, but for a kid from the Bronx with an eighth-grade education, the boom was something that happened to other people. You could get a factory job. You could drive a truck. You could stand at a machine all day and come home smelling like cutting oil. The work had a ceiling, and the ceiling was low.

Between the ages of fourteen and twenty-six, Ralph drifted. At seventeen, he joined the peacetime army for a one-year enlistment — mostly young kids who were floundering, he says — followed by five years in the reserves. When the Korean War started, all his friends were drafted. Ralph wasn't, because he was in the reserves. Just luck. The kind of luck that saves your life without you knowing it at the time.

He worked as a machinist in the garment center, drove a taxi, bounced between factory jobs. He was unreliable, by his own description. He would work for a year, save a little money, then stop working for six months. Women distracted him from going back to night school. He gambled — crap games, poker, the kind of gambling where your knees shake and your heart palpitates and you sweat. He had no plan.

It is worth noting what Ralph does not say about these years: he does not romanticize them. Many men of his generation would later describe their wild years with a wink, a sense of adventure recast in amber. Ralph describes them as what they were — a waste. He was not finding himself. He was losing time. He knew it then, and he knows it now.

During these years, he met Anita. She was fifteen. He was fifteen. She told her friend on a beach, "I'm going to marry that guy."

---


## The Garment Center

I was eighteen years old. I just came out of the army reserves, and I needed a job. And at that point, there were organizations that would help young people with no high school education find work. The place I went to was called the Children's Aid Society, and they sent me to this company called H. Maiman and Company Machinists. They made cutting machines for the garment center.

Now, in the garment center, when they cut cloth, they'd have layers and layers and layers of it stacked up—maybe eight, ten inches high—and they would use a machine with a blade that went up and down. You'd follow a pattern on the top with the blade. But the blade would dull very rapidly, and they'd have to pull it out and sharpen it by hand. H. Maiman made a machine that had automatic sharpeners so the blade came out like a razor blade every time you push the button. That was their thing. That was what made them special.

So they hired me right away even though I had no experience. The way they trained you is they put you in the factory for two weeks, and they showed you how to take the machine apart and put it back together. And then they sent you out into the field.

I'd walk in and people would say, it doesn't cut. Well, I only knew how to take it apart and put it together, so that's what I did. I took it apart. But sometimes I didn't know how to put it back together, so the factory would have to send somebody to fix it. I did this for about two months on and off. I couldn't figure out what was up and what was down. But I finally learned—oh, this is why this doesn't work, that's why that doesn't work. And after a year, I was pretty good at it.

The garment center at that point extended from Fortieth Street and Broadway all the way down to Canal Street. And there were areas that specialized in different kinds of clothing. So when you were uptown, Fortieth Street and Seventh Avenue, that would be very upscale dresses and coats and high-end shirts and blouses. And as you went further and further downtown, you got to places that made housecoats and aprons and things like that. And then when you got all the way down to Canal Street, people were making socks and underwear and stuff that didn't require a lot of design work—not really fashionable. So I'd go from one end of it to the other, fixing cutting machines, and I got to know the whole garment center that way.

The customers liked me. The owner of the company, Meijer Mayman, liked me. But I was not a very responsible person. I was nineteen, I had a little bit of money in my pocket, and I just didn't show up one day. I went on unemployment. And then when I didn't have any money, say a few months later, I would call them. And Meijer Mayman would hire me back.

I would work for a year or two, save a little bit of money, then stop working for six months and go back. They kept on hiring me back. And this went on for four or five years. Maybe six years I worked there, off and on. It was a job where you could only get to a certain level of blue-collar work and maybe make a lower-middle-class living, if that. But I always thought I could go back because Meijer Mayman liked me. I always did a pretty good job when I was there.

And then the last time I went back, he had passed away, and his son had taken over. And his son said, no. We can't hire you. You're totally unreliable.

And that was it. The door closed.

Now, I could have found another factory job. I had bounced around plenty of them—fast food, driving a truck, putting round pegs in round holes. It was mind-numbing. But those were the kinds of jobs you got without an education. Every one of them had a ceiling, and the ceiling was low.

I had tried to go back to school a few times. But some woman would always get in the way and convince me that I'm better off spending the night with her than going to school. And I was easy to convince. I didn't need a lot of convincing. I was young and I had no discipline, and if there was a reason not to go to class, I would take it.

And I was a gambler. Gambling is like an addiction — it is an addiction. I was addicted to it. Here's what I learned about gambling: as a gambler, you can't win. It's not possible. Because in order to get the thrill, you have to gamble for much more than you can afford. So if I was in a crap game and I would hit, I have a thousand dollars — roll the dice, a few seconds, that thousand dollars could be two thousand dollars or could be zero. And if you hit, then you want to up the ante and up the ante until finally you lose. Because the thrill is unbelievable. Your knees shake. Your heart palpitates. You sweat. It's a thrill that's hard to describe. And it's almost impossible to give up.

I remember a poker game — this goes back probably seventy years. We were playing seven-card poker, and my first five cards were three aces and two kings. There was a guy in the game, Chuck Emma, who was definitely a card cheat. He cheated in everything he did. Actually, he was killed by the mafia later because he owed money and went to California to escape, and they found him. So Chuck is raising me. I raise him back. He raises me. I raise him. I'm not going to stop because I know I have him beat. Three aces and two kings — can't possibly lose. Everybody else dropped out. Last card, he gets a straight flush. He cheated. He had cards stacked. I don't know what he did. But that was it. That was the end of it for me. I lost all interest. And now, if I gamble, I find it boring. If I win or lose, it doesn't matter. I'm not gambling for my whole livelihood anymore.

But something about that door closing at H. Maiman—the son looking at me and telling me what I already knew about myself—that stayed with me. I was unreliable. I was a high school dropout bouncing from one dead-end job to the next. I had no plan. I had no future. And I knew it.

I think about those years now, and I don't regret them exactly. I learned a lot in the garment center. I learned how machines work. I learned how to troubleshoot—walk in, figure out why something isn't cutting right, fix it, move on. My brain was doing that kind of thinking for years before I knew there was a name for it. And I learned the city. I walked every block from Fortieth Street to Canal Street and back. I knew the garment center the way a cab driver knows his routes.

But I also learned you can only go so far in these factory jobs without an education. If you were a technical person, you could rise to a managerial position. But if you were just a run-of-the-mill high school dropout, you couldn't. There was nowhere to go.

So when Meijer Mayman's son closed that door on me, he did me a favor. He just didn't know it, and neither did I—not yet.

---

Ralph tells the story of the garment center with remarkable equanimity. He does not blame Meijer Mayman's son for refusing to hire him back. He does not blame himself, exactly, either — though he is honest about his own unreliability in a way that is rare and somewhat disarming. When he says "I was unreliable by nature — or at least by habit," he is making a distinction most people never bother to make. He suspects the flaw was not in his character but in his circumstances, and the decades that followed would prove him right.

The taxi, which he drove while putting himself through college, was the last of his blue-collar jobs — and the one that gave him his most direct encounter with the casual racism of 1950s New York.

---


## Behind the Wheel

I got my license to drive a taxi when I was studying physics in college. I needed a part-time job. My brother-in-law was a cab driver, and he said, get a license and I'll probably be able to get you some work. I said, okay. Great.

In those days — late fifties — licenses were only issued by the police department. So you go down to police headquarters and you're in a big room with lots of people, lots of desks, maybe ten or twenty desks with a policeman at each desk interviewing people for licenses.

Now, my last name is Gorman, and Gorman is an Irish name. And at that point in time, probably the majority of policemen were Irish.

He asks me, where's the Vanderbilt Hotel? I say, Vanderbilt Avenue. He says, don't be a wise guy. What street is it on? What avenue is it near? I say, I don't know. He says, how do you get to LaGuardia Airport from Manhattan? I say, I don't know. Every question he asks me, I say I don't know. Because I didn't know.

So he says, how do you expect to drive a cab if you don't know where anything is? I said, well, what I'm going to do is if somebody gets in the cab and says they want to go to ABC, I'll ask them if they know how to get there. And if they do, I'll take them. And if they don't, I'll ask them to take another cab.

The guy says, okay. You pass.

Now, next to me, there's a Black man applying for a license. He's answering every question correctly. Every one. Except he misses one question out of about fifty. He doesn't pass. He doesn't get his license. I get my license.

We didn't really talk about racism in those days, but it was ever present. That was my first experience really seeing an anti-Black movement by a whole organization. There were very few Black policemen, if any. Very few Black cab drivers. That was racism rearing its ugly head, and that was more than sixty years ago.

And having come from humble beginnings, I was no one to say I'm better than that person. I wasn't. In fact, they were a whole lot better than I was. He knew every answer. I knew nothing. And he didn't get his license.

So now I have a license, but I've never driven a cab before. How it worked was almost like being a longshoreman. You go into a big garage. The regular drivers get their taxis and go out. The rest of us — more people waiting than there are taxis — you have to be assigned one. They picked me to drive because my brother-in-law had a little bit of influence.

I go out and I have no idea what I'm doing. I have no idea how to drop people off. I'm driving in Manhattan. I drive up Forty-Fifth Street and it's one way. I don't understand. How can it be one way when the street next to it goes two ways? I'm all confused. I'm having a horrible time. But I managed to get through my shift, and I made a little bit of money.

Next day, same thing — you have to shape up. They don't pick me. But that's okay. I made a few dollars the day before. Third day, shape up, they pick me. This went on for quite a few months.

They call it hacking. And it's a job that could drive you mad. I'll get one more fare. I'll get one more fare. It just drives you to keep driving and driving until finally you realize your shift is over. And you have to bring in a certain amount of money, otherwise when you shape up the next day, they're not going to hire you. So you really have to hustle all day long.

Once I had a very minor accident — really almost no damage. A fender. Nothing. But they wouldn't give me a taxi for a month after that. One month, no work, because of a fender-bender. That's how it was. You were disposable. There were always more guys waiting to shape up than there were taxis.

It's not a very good job. And nowadays, I feel so sorry for these mostly migrant people who bought their taxicab, and then Uber came in and the licenses are worth nothing. If you own a taxi, you can barely make a living now. It's an awful situation for them.

So I'm glad I'm no longer a cab driver.


---


## The Girl on the Beach

We're doing this story on July fourth because Anita and I met on July fourth, and July always had a very special place in our lives. Very good things happened in July, and very bad things happened in July.

We met on the beach in the Bronx — Orchard Beach — on July fourth weekend. She was with one or two friends, and I was with one or two friends. And we were sharing a blanket and chitchatting. We were both fifteen.

She said to her friend on the beach — she was only fifteen — "I'm gonna marry that guy." And her friend pooh-poohed it or whatever. But Anita had made up her mind.

She lived in the same neighborhood, a few blocks from where I lived. And she came around where I used to hang around at a candy store, and we got to chatting. And then she was having a sweet sixteen party, and I was invited, and I went. And then life went on between us for lots and lots of years.

We met when we were fifteen, and she got married when she was twenty-one. So we went together for six years. We had our ups and downs because I didn't have a job and I didn't have any prospect of ever getting a job. I was a machinist, a taxi driver, bouncing around from one thing to the next. I had no education. I had no plan. And here was this girl who somehow decided at fifteen that I was the one, and she just never changed her mind about it.

Now, Anita was very smart and very talented — I'll talk about that more later. But she came from a lower-middle-class family where the daughters became secretaries and got married. That's what they did. So she was working, and I was bouncing from job to job, and we kept going together through all of it. Six years is a long time when you're young, especially when one of you has no future and the other one is too stubborn to notice.

I think what kept us together was that we genuinely liked each other's company. We were from the same neighborhood, the same kind of people. She knew exactly who I was — an unreliable guy with no education and no prospects — and she didn't care. Or maybe she saw something I didn't see yet. Either way, she stayed.

So we got married, and we didn't have ten cents. I had no job. I had no prospects of ever getting a job because I didn't have a high school diploma. And Anita went to work as a secretary to support us while I tried to figure out what to do with my life.

We got an apartment in the Bronx near Van Cortlandt Park, which was very nice, actually. And between her salary as a secretary and whatever I could bring in driving a taxi or working odd jobs, we made enough of a living. It wasn't much. We were getting along on thirty or forty dollars a week. But it was ours.

And then I went back to night school. I got an academic diploma — a Regents diploma — which meant I could automatically get a place in City University. And Anita supported me through the whole thing. She worked every day so I could go to school full time. No complaints. She helped me with English. She helped me with drafting because she had a very good eye for things. So we had a pretty good relationship while I was in school.

She was the one making it possible. I was the one getting the degree, but she was the one holding everything together. She never said a word about it. She never made me feel like I owed her for it. She just did it. That was Anita. She decided at fifteen she was going to marry me, and once she decided something, that was that.

That's what I think about when I think about Orchard Beach. That fifteen-year-old girl who looked at me on a blanket and told her friend she was going to marry me. She didn't know what she was signing up for. Neither did I. But she was right.

Life went on between us for lots and lots of years.

# Part III: The Transformation

========================================================================


## Editorial Frame


In 1958, the City University of New York charged no tuition. The only cost was four dollars a year in student fees. Books could be borrowed. For the children of immigrants and factory workers, for veterans and late bloomers and anyone who had fallen through the cracks of the American educational system, CUNY was one of the few doors that stayed open regardless of who you were or where you came from. It had been free since 1847 — an experiment in democratic education that would last until 1976, when the city's fiscal crisis ended it. During those years, CUNY produced more Nobel laureates than any public university in the country. It also produced tens of thousands of ordinary professionals — teachers, engineers, accountants, social workers — who would never have had careers without it.

Ralph Gorman walked through that door at twenty-six. He had left school at fourteen. He had spent a dozen years in dead-end jobs. He had driven a taxi, fixed cutting machines, gambled away his paychecks, and watched every opportunity close in front of him. By the time he enrolled, he was not a young man chasing a dream. He was a desperate man with a pregnant wife, and the door Meijer Mayman's son had closed behind him at H. Maiman and Company was the last door he could afford to lose.

He chose physics — not out of passion but out of practicality. This was the Sputnik era. The Soviet Union had put a satellite in orbit, and America was in a panic. Scientists were in demand. Engineers could walk out of college and into jobs. Ralph needed a profession that would pay, and he needed it fast. Anita was eight months pregnant.

What he did not know was that his real obstacle was not physics or chemistry. It was the English language — specifically, his inability to put a sentence together on paper. What happened next became the story he tells more than any other, the one he returns to whenever he wants to explain what he believes about will, and what a person can become if they refuse to accept the verdict the world hands them.

---


## The Summer I Learned to Write

Going from a blue-collar worker to a white-collar worker—that was a big trick. I had no education. I had stopped school when I was about fourteen. I had very poor writing skills, poor speaking skills. I had a good vocabulary because I read a lot, and I was always looking up words in the dictionary. So I could talk fairly well, but I didn't know any grammar. Didn't know much about anything, really, because not going to school was a big handicap.

So when I got into college, I had to take English. And in my first English class, the teacher—who liked me, by the way—he said, "You know, you're never gonna be able to finish college because you don't know how to write a sentence. I don't see how you could possibly get through all your courses without writing a sentence. So I have to give you a D in your first semester of English." He said, "You really should get an F because your papers were awful."

So I said, okay.

Now, I could have quit. I had quit plenty of things before. I was a machinist for H. Maiman and Company in the garment center, and I would work for a year or two, save a little money, then stop working for six months and go back. They kept hiring me back until they didn't. I was unreliable by nature—or at least by habit. So quitting would have been the easy thing.

But something had changed. I was married. Anita was working every day so I could have this chance. I had a baby on the way. And I had run out of blue-collar jobs. I was desperate, and I knew it. I knew exactly what my life would look like if I didn't figure this out.

So then the summer came. I bought a book called *English Usage*. Read the whole book cover to cover. I know eleven places to use a comma. I know how to use dashes, semicolons, colons, everything.

I say that now like it was simple, but it wasn't simple. It was a whole summer of sitting with this book, teaching myself the rules that everybody else had learned in junior high and high school. Things that were second nature to other students—where a period goes, when you use a semicolon instead of a comma, how to break a thought into sentences that make sense on paper—I had to learn all of it from scratch. I was in my late twenties, and I was learning what fourteen-year-olds already knew.

But I learned it.

So then my second semester in English, I got an A. And my third semester, I got an A. And my fourth semester, I got an A. And my papers were being read to the whole class.

I remember that distinctly. The same student whose teacher told him he could never finish college—now the teacher is reading his papers out loud as examples of how to do it right. And I remember thinking: if it's something you wanna do and you have the wherewithal to do it, you can do it.

That became the keynote for everything that came after.

You have to write papers for all your courses except, of course, physics and mathematics. You don't usually write papers in those. So you have to write a lot of papers, and being really good in English made it much easier for me to write papers in history and psychology, political science, or whatever courses I had to take. English was the keynote for me for all my courses except mathematics and physics and chemistry.

And the funny thing about those subjects—math, physics, chemistry—I turned out to be good at them. Really good. It's like my mind had been waiting for that kind of work. All those years I was fixing cutting machines in the garment center, figuring out why a machine wouldn't cut straight, troubleshooting problems on the factory floor—my brain was already doing that kind of thinking. I just didn't know there was a name for it.

So I studied. I studied almost all the time. Studying became a way of life for me. We lived in an apartment in Manhattan, and the walls were made of tissue paper. My children had to learn how to watch TV like most people would be lip reading—that's how low the volume was, because I had to have absolute quiet. Otherwise, I couldn't study. Eventually, I started going to the public library, or to Columbia, which had a very impressive collection of physics and engineering books. So I was either studying at home or studying at one of the libraries.

And I didn't stop. I went to night school for fifteen years. Not because I had to, but because I loved it. I got a master's in engineering and a master's in physics. I went from a man who couldn't write a sentence to a man with two graduate degrees.

Sometimes I think about that teacher who gave me the D. He wasn't wrong. At that moment, I couldn't write. My papers were awful. He was telling the truth. But he was wrong about what I could become. He looked at where I was and assumed that was where I'd stay. And I understand that—most people do stay where they are. Most people don't buy a grammar book and read it cover to cover over a summer because a teacher told them they were hopeless.

But I did. And if it's something you wanna do, and you have the wherewithal to do it, you can do it.

---

There is a pattern in Ralph Gorman's life that becomes visible only in retrospect. When he encounters a gap in his knowledge — whether it is English grammar or semiconductor physics or how to drive a tractor in a straight line — his response is always the same: he buys a book, he reads it cover to cover, and he teaches himself. It is a method born of necessity. He had no mentors, no professional network, no family tradition of education to draw on. What he had was stubbornness and time, and he applied both in equal measure.

The summer he taught himself to write was the first and most dramatic instance of this pattern. But it would not be the last. In the memoir that follows, Ralph describes the transition that changed his life overnight — and the years of self-education that made it possible.

---


## From Taxi to Transistor

I never thought about going to school until I was literally desperate. I had run out of blue-collar jobs. I was driving a taxi, driving a truck, working in various factories, putting round pegs in round holes. It was mind-numbing. And so I decided at that point I had to change my life.

This was the Sputnik era, and scientists were in great demand. I needed a profession that would allow me to go to work and get a job immediately. I already had a baby on the way. If they would have said door catchers wanted, I would have been a door catcher. But they always advertised engineering, physics, chemistry. Those were the big fields.

On Friday — I remember this distinctly — I was driving a taxi. My wife was in her eighth month. She had just stopped work that weekend. They were calling me to pick up fares. And on Monday, I was wearing a suit, and I was an engineer. That was the transition. Friday, a cab. Monday, a suit.

I went to work for United Technologies in a research lab where they were making semiconductors. And at that point, semiconductors were in their infancy. Every step, no matter how trivial, an engineer would do. So I spent six months on the first step, six months on the second. I spent a year learning how to polish a crystal. One year till we got it perfect. Then a few months in this area, a few months in that area. And so I had an overall picture of every single step to make a semiconductor — from the growth of the crystal all the way to putting it in a package.

By the time I finished five years, everybody was a specialist. There was a specialist in the first step, a specialist in the second step, all the way to the very end. But there were very few people who had an overall view of the whole process. And that served me all during my consulting days. I could look at a chip and say, well, it's more likely the problem is in this area or that area.

I loved engineering because every day, there would be a new puzzle to solve. You'd get this assignment and work on it. It was like every day you have to work on this puzzle, make this thing work. I could spend three, four, five, six months on one puzzle. Then you finish that puzzle, they give you another. And there was a group of about eight of us — no one ever left to take another job because we all kind of fit in with each other. The supervisor was a super smart guy, and we all liked him.

The thing I didn't like was the uncertainty. Working for United Technology, they lose a helicopter contract in Tanganyika, and ten people have to be fired. It's hit or miss. When that happened, everybody started getting their résumés together. You don't know if you're the one until it happens. There were always two weeks where the rumors were flying. And it was almost every year. I managed to escape all of them, but I said, I don't want to live like this for my whole life.

I remember an engineer there who'd been there fifteen years. The following year, he was going to get his pension guaranteed — I don't remember what it's called. He was fired just before he was eligible. He was crying. And I said, I don't want to go through this again. So I left.

But I carried everything I'd learned there with me. That six years in the research lab, learning every step, gave me something most people didn't have. And later, when I became a consultant, it was the reason companies kept calling.

# Part IV: The Working Life

========================================================================


## Editorial Frame


Ralph's career after United Technology split into two parallel tracks, and the combination was unusual enough that it bears explaining.

He became a physics professor at Bronx Community College, teaching twelve hours a week — thirty weeks a year, with twenty-two weeks off. Community colleges in the 1960s and 1970s were the workhorses of American higher education, open to anyone, affordable to everyone, staffed by professors who were often more devoted to teaching than to research. The pay was poor. When Ralph started, engineers were making twenty thousand dollars a year; teachers were making six thousand. But the schedule was extraordinary. Ralph took a sabbatical every three years — at no pay, or at half pay — because he could afford to. He could afford to because of his other career.

He became a semiconductor consultant, working one or two days a week for companies that needed problems solved. The semiconductor industry in the 1960s and 1970s was still young enough that a man who had spent six years learning every step of chip manufacturing — from crystal growth to packaging — was a rare and valuable thing. Most engineers had become specialists. Ralph had an overall view. When a production line went wrong, he could look at a chip and narrow the problem to a specific step, because he had done every step himself.

The teaching gave him time — time to think, to travel, to raise his children, to take his daughter to the beach every day all summer. The consulting gave him money and intellectual challenge. Together, they let him build a life that a dropout machinist from the Bronx was never supposed to have.

---


## Twelve Hours a Week

A lot of my friends who had gone back to school became teachers because it was pretty simple. You got a college degree, took some education courses, and got a job. The pay was awful — if I was making twenty thousand as an engineer, teachers were making six thousand. But they seemed to have a nice life.

I taught math in a vocational high school for one semester. Pretty boring, actually. Then I got a job teaching chemistry in an academic high school, Evander Childs High School. The chairman liked me a lot. The physics teacher was retiring, and I already had a master's in physics. He promised me tenure — a lifetime job. But I didn't really like it that much. Kind of interesting, but a little boring. So I decided I had to leave.

I went back to engineering at IBM. I was only there a month. I had applied for jobs at two community colleges in New York City. I got both jobs and picked the one closest to the house.

And I loved teaching physics. It was fabulous. It was a fabulous life. You teach thirty weeks a year. You're off completely for twenty-two weeks. You only teach twelve hours a week, so you never work five days a week. And teaching physics was an interesting subject. You get some interesting questions. You learn a little bit as you're teaching. It was challenging.

What made it different from high school was that the students who were taking physics needed it for some career they were going into. If you want to be a pharmacist, you need a year of physics. If you want to be an engineer, you need two years. So you're always challenged by the students to do a little bit more.

I became a pretty good teacher by following the lead of really good teachers — watching what they do, how relaxed they were in the classroom. I always made sure I was there five or ten minutes early. And then I noticed if I was there earlier, they were there earlier, and they'd ask me questions about what we were going to do for the day.

I was always a little outspoken at staff meetings. One day, they're talking about how all the city colleges have to accept all the credits the junior colleges are offering. People get up and say, we can teach advanced psychological techniques, we can teach advanced chemistry, advance this, advance that. I get up and I say, you know what this means? We could teach the last two years of college first.

The whole place breaks up. The deans hate me.

And one day, because they knew that whenever I spoke, people were going to laugh — this woman gets up. You have to give your name and department. She gets up and says, her name is Ralph Gorman, and she's in the physics department. And she goes on to say the most outrageous thing. The whole auditorium erupts.

I was on a committee on instruction. I would go away on sabbatical for a year. I come back a year later, they're still talking about the same thing.

I formed one close friendship at the college — the chairman of the physics department. He was brilliant. We used to have long conversations. When he was about sixty, he married a woman who had been a nun, and he was reluctant about the whole thing. I kind of convinced him it was the best thing that ever happened to him. They made a camper out of a van and traveled all over America. And then, unfortunately, when he turned sixty-five, he got a very serious cancer, and he died. That was the only real friendship I ever formed through school or work.

Every three years, I would take a sabbatical at no pay. Every six years, a sabbatical at half pay. Most teachers couldn't afford it because they didn't have another income. But I had my consulting salary. So every three years, I was off a year. An amazing experience.

When you're away for two months and then you come back, you can think about teaching a little bit because you're not under pressure. You can improve your skills by reading, maybe taking a refresher course somewhere. So the sabbaticals were, for me, like a refresher.

And during those sabbaticals, my wife and I traveled. We went on cruises before cruises were popular — a cruise to Australia on a tiny ship with a hundred passengers. We rented an apartment in Italy. We rented an apartment in Spain. We rented an apartment in Cuba. My wife was an adventuresome traveler. She would want to go somewhere nobody else has been. We took a cruise to Bali, picked up the ship, and went across the Indian Ocean to Thursday Island — the northernmost tip of Australia. Aborigines there are different from the ones on the mainland because there's five hundred miles of distance. The island had a Japanese pearl-diving community. They'd built fortifications because they thought the Japanese were going to attack, but it turned out a Japanese princess was living there, so that never happened.

My wife did a lot of the research. I would just take off.

And because I only taught thirty weeks a year and was off for twenty-two weeks, I had a lot of time with my children. I was like a major caregiver, especially with my daughter Rena. My wife went back to work, and I was off all summer. Rena loved to go to the beach every day. She was two, three years old, and I couldn't read at the beach because you have to keep your eye on her — she goes into the water. You can't get involved in a book. So I hated the beach. She wanted to go at nine o'clock, and I'd say, oh, the beach isn't open yet. The lifeguard isn't there. And she never wanted to come home for lunch. She wanted to just stay there, not eat, just stay at the beach. On the weekends, my wife would say, do you want to go to the beach? I'd say, no. I never want to go to the beach again. I've had enough beach to last me a lifetime. But Rena and I have a great relationship, actually.

I don't like working every day. I like working two or three days a week. I like being off, so I have time to think. When you're working continuously, all you think about is your work. I like to think about things I'd like to do, places I'd like to see.


---


## The Consultant

When I went to work for a small company after United Technology, I did a really good job for them. I made them a chip that made a million dollars, and they liked me. They gave me a contract. They gave me stock options.

And that's when I was teaching, which I really needed because my salary as a teacher was so poor. A friend of mine who had a small company said, we have a perfect summer job for you. So I went to work for him. And then another company called me. And another. Before I knew it, I had a small consulting business.

Then I got a job at General Instrument on Long Island. They wanted me to make something that looked pretty straightforward. I spent a year on it, gave them a sample. They said, why don't you stay on? We have another project for you. I said, okay.

I had a boss there, Joe Entrada, who was brilliant. He was a genius. We kind of complemented each other — whatever he didn't know, I knew. So together, we were an unbeatable combination. I remember we did a job together, and the president of the company says, you guys have lifetime jobs. You guys are the tops.

The president was fired a year later. And we were back in the pool again.

I was at General Instrument as a consultant, which is highly unusual, for seventeen years. When I'd finish a project, they'd give me another project. And another. And then finally, they actually closed the plant, and that was it.

The most rewarding thing about consulting was going into a company that was in deep trouble — a problem that their crew of engineers couldn't solve. I remember one company in Westchester. They had made a small change in their process. All the indications were that there was no change they could see. Electrically, it was identical to what they were producing. But there was a physical change they didn't pick up.

I had to measure electric currents in the picoamp range — ten to the minus twelfth. Fractions of that. I had no idea how to do it, but they had an engineer who set me up with the testing equipment. And then I was able to track the process all the way to the step where they made the change. And I realized the change was physical, and that physical change ruined their whole process.

I solved that problem. I thought they would love me — they were going to go out of business. I had a direct line to the president, everything. I thought I was a big hero and they'd probably want to keep me on.

They wouldn't talk to me again. They ran three runs. Every one was perfect. They paid off my contract, and they never spoke to me again.

The engineers didn't like me. They poisoned the atmosphere — oh, he's going to steal our secrets, he's going to take it to the competitor.

So here's what I learned about consulting: if you do a great job, they throw you out. And if you do a lousy job, well, you deserve to be thrown out. So either way, you're out.

I remember having consulting jobs where I knew I could do the work easily, and they'd just offer me a contract but never sign it. Just keep questioning and questioning. So I'd give them more advice and more advice. After about a month, I'd say, where's my contract? And they'd say, well, we decided not to give it to you. A month of free advice. Saved them millions of dollars.

A couple of the jobs I did were very successful, and I was very pleased with myself. Even though the company fired me anyhow. But that was the nature of it.

I never would take a consulting job that I didn't think I could do. If I thought it was too complicated, too intricate — I didn't take it.

And I always told the truth. I learned that early. There was a math professor at the college who was brilliant. We used to have long conversations. One day, I said to him, well, you know, I think you should do A, B, C about the math department. And he said to me — and this was really a challenge — he said, well, did you just think of that, or did you hear it from somebody? Oh my god. I had to own up. I said, I heard it from somebody. And he said, I'm not asking you who you heard it from. So I learned early on: tell the truth. It's the easiest way out. If you tell a lie, you have to remember how to back it up. If you tell the truth, you never have to worry.


---

The consulting work took Ralph to places he never expected. For a boy who had grown up on a single block in the Bronx — where the farthest journey was the subway to the garment center — the world opened in stages. First the drive to Long Island for General Instrument. Then companies scattered across the Northeast. Then Taiwan, where the semiconductor industry was booming and where an American consultant who understood every step of chip manufacturing was worth his weight in transistors. Ralph's mother had given him an ear for languages and the confidence to walk into any country and make himself understood. Now, forty years later, her gift found its practical use.

---


## The World Opens

My first trip to Taiwan — I can't remember the dates exactly, probably in the eighties — everybody was riding a bicycle. Except for a few motor scooters, it was all bicycles. The second time I went back, there were no more bicycles. Everybody was on a motor scooter, and there were a few cars. And the third time I went back, there were very few motor scooters and mainly everybody driving a car. So that was Taiwan really opening up.

The trip I want to tell you about, I had to go to Taiwan on business. My partner, Muni Mitchell, was a big executive in the company I was consulting for, and he wanted his wife to go so Anita would have company. So we all went to Taiwan together.

Now, when we arrived, they expected me to go to the factory and do whatever I was supposed to do. But I was jet-lagged. I couldn't even think straight. It takes me a week or more to get out of the jet lag, so I wasn't going to bother going to the factory because I'd fall asleep. So Anita says, let's go to this temple of a thousand steps. I said, okay.

The way you traveled through Taiwan in those days is you told the head man at the hotel where you wanted to go, and he would fill out a card in Chinese, and you would give that to the taxi driver, and they would take you to that destination. So we get to this temple, about an hour outside of Taipei. Beautiful. We climb the thousand steps. And on the way up, there are all kinds of places — some little side temples that you could go in and look at. We spent about two hours climbing to the top. And then somebody at the top said, you know, there's a bird sanctuary another few steps up. So we said, oh, we'd love to see that. And we went up — Anita and I and Muni's wife — and we spent about an hour. Most exotic birds. Beautiful.

So then we say, it's time to go home. We have to find a taxi. We walked down the stairs, but there were so many different sets of stairs, and we wound up on a dirt road. We didn't know where we were. They look at me. I look at them. I say, which way? I said, I don't know. Let's just keep walking. There's only two ways you can go.

So we started walking, and then a Chinese woman runs up behind us. She's saying something and pointing in the other direction. We keep walking. She's following us, saying, "Taipei!" and pointing the other way. Finally, I look up ahead, and there's nothing up there at all. So I say, okay. Let's go the other way.

We turn around and start walking on this dirt road. We get to a little entryway with steps. She points to the steps and says, "Taipei." So we don't know what we're doing, but we climb these steps, and we come out in a bus terminal with a bus waiting a few feet away that says Taipei. We jump on the bus. We have some Chinese money. And we go straight back to the hotel.

So here's an interesting story about the hotel. Muni Mitchell's wife — I can't remember her name — was Irish and had a temper like you can't believe. The next day, I come down, and Muni's moping. I say, what's wrong? He says, we had an argument last night, and she picked up the telephone and threw it through a big mirror on the wall. He says, I don't know how to handle this. The Chinese don't do those things. I said, well, here's what you tell them. You say your wife got up in the middle of the night and the phone rang and she picked it up to answer it and she tripped. The phone flew out of her hand and hit the mirror. He says, I'll do that.

So he's telling this to the man at the desk. Meanwhile, the man is looking at him like, you must be crazy to make me believe that story. But he just says, oh, okay. We'll replace it tomorrow.

So we leave, and we take a little plane to the very tip of Taiwan. And we went to an area called Taroko Gorge. It's a national park. And I'm looking and there are mountains, mountain after mountain, of just pure jade. And temples built into the mountain, made of jade. It's the most amazing thing. We spent hours there.

We come out. Of course there's a gift shop. They're haggling about buying some jade. I don't buy anything. I like to go to a real store.

So we go back to the hotel. I say I'd like to have the same room. No problem. Muni says, I want the same room I had. And the man says, oh, no. You can't have that room. Muni says, why can't I have it? He says, bad luck.

That was my trip to Taiwan. We spent about two weeks. Anita and I and Muni's wife went everywhere while he went to the factory to play engineer. We had a great time.

# Part V: The People

========================================================================


## Editorial Frame


The previous sections of this book have been about what Ralph Gorman did — the jobs, the education, the career he built from nothing. This section is about the people he did it with and for.

Ralph married Anita when she was twenty-one and he had nothing — no degree, no job, no prospects. She worked as a secretary to support them both while he went to school. She never complained. When he graduated and their income jumped tenfold overnight, she reinvented herself — first as an artist, then as a potter, then as a chef. She was always becoming something new.

They raised three children in the Bronx and Manhattan. Peter and Eric went to the Bronx High School of Science and then to CUNY Albany. Rena, ten years younger, grew up as the baby sister with two near-adult brothers watching over her. Ralph gave them all extraordinary freedom — a deliberate reaction, perhaps, to the household he had grown up in, where freedom was the only thing his parents had given him.

And throughout all of it, Ralph maintained friendships that stretched back to childhood — some lasting over ninety years. In their fifties, after decades of separation, the old group from the Bronx reconvened. They went on cruises together. They traveled to Barcelona, Greece, Croatia, Corsica. They picked up the friendship they had set aside in their thirties, and it was as if no time had passed at all. Most of them are gone now.

---


## Anita in Full

Anita was a very, very talented artist. Her art teacher in high school said she definitely should go to art school because she had an innate talent. With a pencil, she could draw a likeness of anybody in a few seconds. You would say, she would pick up a pencil, sketch pad, and in ten seconds, you would know exactly who she drew. She did sketches of all my friends, sketches of me — charcoal or pencil on paper. Everybody knew exactly who they were. There wasn't any question about it.

She was accepted to all the nice art schools in New York. But her father said, just go out and get a job. You don't want to be an artist. In those days, lower-middle-class families didn't want their daughters to be artists. They wanted them to be secretaries. So that was that.

She didn't take up painting until we were married. Then she took up watercolors, went on to oils, did some pottery. She worked her way through the whole artistic world. She took a lot of art classes. She went to art school for a year. And we had a friend who was a professional artist, and he actually came to the house once or twice a week. Anita studied under him for a year or two. Then she broke out and started doing everything on her own.

She learned how to throw beautiful pots — really beautiful. She taught herself how to mix the glazes and do all of the things that go into turning out good pottery. Then she did some beautiful oils. Her paintings — we still have them. My children have her paintings in their houses. They're good. You look at them and you know she had talent.

She really grew into her artist. She had the freedom to do that once we had money. For years, she had been working as a secretary to support me while I went to school. She helped me with English. She helped me with drafting because she had a very good eye for things. No complaints, ever. And then our life changed when I graduated — from thirty or forty dollars a week to three hundred dollars a week. It was a gigantic change.

We moved from the Bronx to Manhattan. They were trying to bring families back, and the city was practically giving away apartments — three bedrooms, two baths, a garage in the building, an eighteen-foot terrace overlooking Central Park, on Columbus Avenue. She was afraid to move. Too much crime. How are the kids going to do in school? On and on. I said, we're going to try it. If we don't like it, we'll move on.

She loved it from day one. She couldn't get enough of it. She had a very good friend in the building. They explored all the restaurants and the museums and the parks. On the weekends when I was free, she knew where to go and how to get there. Take the bus everywhere. She loved living in Manhattan.

And then she went from artist to chef. She went to cooking school for six months, got a job as a sous chef somewhere, and wound up as the manager of a fancy gourmet shop on Madison Avenue. She was there for about five years until the store closed. Then she went to work for Zabar's brother, who had a shop on Madison Avenue. She worked for him as a chef.

She evolved as a chef over quite a few years. She evolved as an artist over quite a few years. She was always becoming something new. She never stopped.

And when I told her I wanted to be a farmer, she said, what do you know about farming? And I said, I don't know anything about farming, but I know how to read, I know how to think. So she came along. I'll tell you more about that later.

She was my childhood sweetheart. We went together for six years before we got married. She was the one who, when we were fifteen years old on a beach, told her friend she was going to marry me. She was right about that, and she was right about most things.


---

Ralph and Anita both came from households where children were essentially unsupervised — raised, as Ralph puts it, with benign neglect. Both had left school early. Both had parents who worked factory jobs and paid little attention to what their children did or became. And yet the family they built together was not a repetition of what they had known. They gave their children freedom — a great deal of it, by any standard — but it was deliberate freedom, not neglect. The difference, perhaps, is that Ralph and Anita were paying attention. They just chose not to interfere.

---


## Raising New Yorkers

We've had pretty good luck with our children. They all wound up to be pretty well contented people.

They were born in the Bronx. Van Cortlandt Park was right across the street from our apartment — just a short block and you were in the park. And if we wanted to go to Manhattan, the train was only half a block from the house. So they had a pretty contented childhood.

Then we moved to Manhattan, and they had to go to public school. At that point, Manhattan was a pretty rough place. Schools were not in great shape. A lot of tough kids. They had to learn how to adapt pretty fast, otherwise they'd be in trouble. There were blocks that you couldn't walk down, so you had to take a circuitous route to school. Sometimes they would come home without their sneakers — somebody stole them or forced them to give them up.

But the one nice thing: they could walk to see their friends, or take a bus right outside the apartment. As opposed to the suburbs where you need somebody to drive you, they didn't need much supervision. They knew how to use the subway, the buses. Most of the time, they could walk ten blocks to see their friends. We had a very nice apartment — three bedrooms. My sons Peter and Eric each had their own room.

Both of them went to the Bronx High School of Science. They got a pretty good education in the public school system, which was unusual at that point. Then they both went to CUNY Albany. Peter majored in accounting. Eric majored in management information systems. They both came out and got jobs right away.

Rena was ten years younger. She was born while the boys were in college. Peter and Eric both played a fatherly role with her. They took her under their wing, made sure she was always safe. They'd babysit so we could go out. And even today, the three of them are very close. They spend a lot of time together.

We gave them a lot of freedom to do whatever they wanted, as long as we thought they'd be safe. And in their teenage years, they could go to a party and come back whenever the party was over. We'd stay up and wait for them, always a little nervous about where they were and who they were with.

I remember one particular incident where Peter didn't come home one night. He was sixteen or so. We were beside ourselves. We couldn't call his friends at four in the morning. We're waiting until a reasonable hour. And he walks in around seven in the morning. My wife jumps up and hugs him. Where have you been? I say, what's wrong with you? Why didn't you call us?

He says, well, I was with this girl. What should I say, I have to call my mommy?

So we realized at sixteen, you're almost an adult. We gave them a lot of freedom. Eric also — if he had a girlfriend and wanted to bring her home and stay over, that was okay with us. And we did the same thing with Rena. My wife was somewhat reluctant about giving Rena all that freedom. But I said, so do you want her to have sex in a remote place, in a car somewhere? Isn't it better that she's in a safe place? And I think my wife came around to understanding that you're not going to stop them. You might as well try to promote it in the safest way possible.

I think I influenced their careers a little. One of them asked, what should I major in? I said, if you major in sciences, you're guaranteed a job. If you major in accounting, you're probably guaranteed a job. Peter took that advice. He became a CPA. He bounced from job to job for a while, but now he's a top executive in the reinsurance industry. One of the leading figures in the country, actually.

Rena was in college and she wanted to come home. Didn't want to stay. I said, look, it's your decision. You want to come home, you can come home. You'll be a waitress. Is that what you're doing? She said, I don't want to be a waitress. I said, so you've got a choice. And she became a registered dietitian. She works with children who have eating disorders. Teaches them how to cook healthy meals. She's been on TV.

And Eric — I had a little influence at General Instrument. I wasn't part of the inner circle, but I was well up there. So when Eric was looking for a job in MIS, I asked if there were any openings. When you're recommended by the president of the company, you're pretty much in. He got the job, and the interesting thing was nobody knew he was my son for about a year. They just called him Eric, called me Ralph. We both had the same last name, Gorman, but nobody put it together. One day, a vice president says to me, wow, we have this young man we hired. He's so smart. He's working out so well. I said, yeah, what's his name? They said, Eric. I said, oh, that's my son. Oh my god. No wonder he's so smart. He spent quite a few years at General Instrument and learned a lot. That's what took him on to further his career.

My grandson Dylan used to work at the farm when he was twelve. He was standing at the door of the barn selling raspberries, wearing a Cornell sweatshirt. A woman walked in and said, oh, do you want to go to Cornell? He was twelve. He said, oh, no. I already graduated.

We haven't gotten a job for Zoe yet, but she's only a couple of months old.

## Old Friends

I had about six or seven very close friends when I grew up. Some of them I knew for over ninety years because we lived in the same building in the Bronx. And then as we aged, got into our thirties, got married, had children, we all separated and didn't see each other. But then in our fifties, maybe even a little later, we got very close again. We started seeing each other frequently. We traveled together. We went on cruises. And we reestablished that old relationship, which really never disappeared, that we had when we were children.

Most of them never went to college. Out of about twenty-five people we were in contact with in the neighborhood, only three of us went to school — me, my friend Neil, and my friend Herbie. The others, for the most part, unless their father owned a business, all became hairdressers. In those days, you could be a hairdresser in three months, and then you go to work and make a living right away. And most of them made a very successful living out of it. Most of them owned their own shop after a little while.

But it's a strange business. You're always acting. You're, say, a handsome thirty-five, forty-year-old man, and you're coloring a woman's hair who's in her eighties, and she wants to be complimented, and you have to play the part. So you're always acting as a hairdresser. You're playing a part most of the time. You're not your real self. And it can be wearing. Most of them eventually gave it up. One became a taxi driver. One owned a restaurant. One went into construction. But they all started as hairdressers. I thought that was kind of like a strange period in my life when ten of my friends were hairdressers.

So I'd like to talk about each one because they're still in my memory bank. Most of them are dead now except for one who lives in New Jersey.

---

My friend Herbie was my best friend. We grew up in the same neighborhood, and I think we were both risk-takers. He was in the navy. I was in the army. And when he came out of the navy, he was about twenty, and we became very close.

Now, I have to tell an interesting story about Herbie. He was stationed in Hawaii, and for some reason he got involved with some people on shore and people in the navy, and they were doing some pretty bad drugs. And to get the money for the drugs, he would be selling off some of what he had. Somehow or other he got caught, and they were going to put him in jail for ten years. His father sent a lawyer to Hawaii, paid for everything. The lawyer appeared at his court-martial and somehow managed to get him a general discharge, not a dishonorable discharge. So he was eligible for the GI Bill. He did a year in jail in Hawaii, he came home clean as a whistle, never wanted to look at a drug again after that horrible experience.

So he went back to school, got a degree in mathematics, became a teacher. And that's how I went into teaching — because Herbie was a teacher and he seemed to like it very much. I kind of followed in his footsteps.

He moved to Manhattan from the Bronx, and then about a year later I moved around the corner from him. This was the early seventies, and the neighborhood was awful. Crime was rampant. If you parked your car on the street, you could be sure it was broken into that night. So if I ever had to park my car on the street — I had a garage in the building, which was lucky — I'd leave everything open and a note that says everything is open. So they could open the doors, look in the glove compartment, open everything. The only hope was they didn't take the battery.

I remember one summer, we must have been in our thirties. He had two children, three and four. I had two children, two and three. And we shared a bungalow in Monroe, New York. It was a very small kitchen, two bedrooms. Four people in each bedroom. No living room. Just two bedrooms and a kitchen. But we spent most of our time outside, and they had entertainment on Saturday nights — dancing, telling jokes. I became kind of the life of the party. We did that for two weeks, and he said, well, are we going this Saturday? I said, no. That's it. I'm done. So we learned how to play bridge for the rest of the summer.

His wife and my wife became friends. We'd go out together, all four of us. We were friendly right up until the time he died, which was four or five years ago. We were like twins. I have pictures of him and me walking down the road here, arms around each other's shoulders. So I miss him, actually.

---

My friend Neil — I was the best man at his wedding. My wife was the maid of honor. So we have a relationship that goes way, way back to when we were ten or twelve years old.

Neil went back to school at night, and it took him twelve years to get his bachelor's degree. Then he got a degree in engineering. And this is very coincidental, but we wound up working for the same small company. I was a consultant, and he was the quality control manager. So we loved it because we would chitchat and go for lunch. We weren't just good friends — we were colleagues.

Then he started his own electronics business, selling semiconductors to the government. And I got connected to that business because I was still doing semiconductor work. We actually bought an inventory of chips from General Instrument — chips on a wafer, which are useless unless they're tested and put in a package. If they were in packages, they'd be worth millions. In chip form, they were worthless.

So my job was to go to Taiwan and get them tested and packaged. I went out to a factory outside Taipei. The engineers knew what they were doing. I told them what I wanted, and over lunch I said, so what's it going to cost me? He says, how many do you want? I said, fifty thousand. Twenty-five cents each. I didn't even negotiate. I said okay. So for twenty-five cents, we were making chips we could sell for two dollars. The first order, all of a sudden we're splitting fifty thousand dollars. Then we're splitting a hundred thousand. One year, I made two hundred thousand dollars, and this was in the seventies. That was a lot of money. I wound up setting up a defined benefits plan, and the whole thing went in tax-free. Boy, did that accumulate.

---

My friend Jackie I met in the neighborhood when I was twelve or thirteen. During the Korean War, almost all of my friends were in either the coast guard, the navy, or the army. There wasn't anybody around except me, because I was in the reserves and wasn't drafted. Jackie was in the navy but stationed in Brooklyn at the navy yard, and his job was to be a driver for the admiral. If the admiral was away for a week or two, Jackie would be free and clear. So we spent a lot of time together.

He was an interesting guy. Never went to school, but a great speaker. He could convince you of anything — kind of like a con artist.

After the navy, he became a hairdresser, like everybody else. Then he moved to Florida. He became a political person down there — the water commissioner for Broward County. And when he walked into a restaurant or any place of business, they would jump up and do whatever he wanted because he was in charge of assigning water rights. He was really way up on the ladder. Water commissioners are pretty high.

But somebody said to him, you know, you should run for mayor. And the mayor was one of his friends — the one who'd appointed him. So Jackie ran against the mayor and lost, and that was the end of his political career. The mayor fired him, so he's no longer the water commissioner. And he wound up doing essentially menial jobs after that.

---

And then there was my friend Davey. Davey didn't graduate high school, but he was very smart. He owned a frankfurter business at Jamaica Station, and it was a very, very successful business. But he always despaired that he never had an education.

So it was Davey, Neil, Herbie, and Jackie. And in our fifties, we all reconnected. We took a cruise to Barcelona. We took a cruise to Greece. We took a trip to Corsica. We went to Croatia. We really loved being together. All our meals were together. We went on day trips together. It was quite an experience.

And now most of them are gone. But that old relationship that we had when we were children — it never really disappeared. We just put it aside for a while, and then we picked it back up, and it was like no time had passed at all.

---

Ralph does not dwell on loss. He mentions that Herbie died, that Jackie's career collapsed, that Davey never got over his lack of education — and then he moves on. The tone is matter-of-fact in the way that very old people sometimes are: not because they don't feel the losses, but because they have felt so many that grief has become a familiar room they enter and leave without ceremony.

What is striking about this memoir is not what Ralph says about his friends but what he reveals about himself. He was the risk-taker. He was the one who went back to school, who guided the young farmers on what to grow, who walked into companies and solved problems that entire engineering teams could not. For a man who spent twelve years describing himself as unreliable, Ralph Gorman became someone other people relied on.

# Part VI: The Land

========================================================================


## Editorial Frame


In the 1980s, Ralph decided he wanted to be a farmer.

It is worth pausing on the distance between the boy who grew up on 167th Street in the Bronx — where every apartment building held twenty kids and nobody had a yard — and the man who bought seventeen acres of overgrown Connecticut farmland surrounded by a land trust. That distance is not just geographical. Ralph had no model for this. His parents were factory workers. His neighbors were factory workers. Nobody he knew as a child had ever grown anything. The closest he had come to agriculture was pushing a cart through the garment center.

But Ralph Gorman's method was, as always, the same. He had taught himself to write by reading a grammar book. He had taught himself semiconductor physics by spending a year on each step. Now he would teach himself to farm — first by befriending a neighbor who knew things he did not, and then by buying a tractor, reading the manual, and practicing in a barn all winter.

The land he found was in Sherman, Connecticut, part of the rural northwest corner of the state where dairy farms had been the way of life for over a century. By the 1980s, most of those farms were struggling or gone. The fields that had once fed forty cows were being carved into lots by land trusts or left to grow over with six-foot weeds. Ralph bought the biggest lot — seventeen acres with a working barn, a silo, a pond, and a view. What followed was a second life.

---


## Learning from Jack

My first house in Connecticut was a very small summer cottage, but it had a spectacular view of Kenilwood Lake. The house itself was built as a summer cottage, and it kind of remained as a summer cottage. They put in a heating system, and they made a big addition at the end. But in the winter, if you went from the living room to the back bedroom, you had to put on a jacket. A twenty-five-degree change in temperature just going to the back of the house. So we mainly used it as our summer house. The kids had a great time there because we had access to Candlewood Lake and the beach.

My neighbor lived walking distance through the woods, maybe a couple hundred feet through a little wooded path. His name was Jack Doobie. He was an elementary school science teacher, and he knew a lot. I mean, he was like a renaissance man. He knew how to grow raspberries. He knew how to pick mushrooms. He knew how to fish. He knew how to hunt. He knew all these things, and I was from New York City. I didn't know any of those things.

So he taught me quite a bit. He taught me all about how to pick mushrooms, how to identify them. We used to go on mushroom hikes together, and then my wife became an avid mushroom collector. She had giant books on mushrooms, how to identify them. We made it a point not to eat white mushrooms because the amanita, which is quite poisonous — if you take one bite, there's no cure, you're dead. So we stayed away from white mushrooms. But Jack was very brave. We were eating ten or fifteen kinds of wild mushrooms from the field — shelf mushrooms, chicken mushrooms. I can't even remember how many we ate, but at least fifteen different types.

After a rain, you'd go into the woods, and it was interesting to go with him because he would point out things. He'd say, see if you look there, you'll see something interesting. And there'd be some wildlife or some mushroom growing out of a log. He had an eye for it.

He taught me how to camp. I never went camping, so he showed me how to build fires, how to set up tents. He taught me about planting, nurturing, weeding, composting. We make an enormous amount of compost now. We try to use as much compost as we can instead of using herbicides. He was all into all natural. If you could do it naturally, that's the way to do it.

And he taught me what I think is the most important thing about farming: if you look at it not as a series of problems but as a series of challenges, it takes on a new role. You want to work with nature, not against nature.

His daughter and my daughter Rena became very good friends. They were like best of friends, and Rena loved spending the summer there.

I remember one time, Jack said he wanted to try growing strawberries. I said, fine. I'll go out in the field and plow up a two-hundred-foot row. You can plant strawberries and see what you can do. He loved the experience, but the work was overwhelming. Strawberries are on the ground, and they're susceptible to everything. If it rains, they get full of mud and they spoil. If it rains and then it's cloudy, they spoil. Then you've got the chipmunks. The chipmunks love strawberries, but what they like to do is take a bite out of each one. Not the whole thing. If they ate five strawberries, I would be happy. But they bite into fifty, take a little bite of each one.

So he had to put Reemay on them, which is a cover that allows air and water to get in but doesn't allow bugs and animals. But you have to pin it down so the chipmunks can't get under it. It was an ordeal. He did it for one season.

Our friendship changed when I bought the farm a few miles away. I was really busy — busy, busy, busy all day long. He would still come and visit, and we'd talk about farming. But our bond seemed different once I was on my own land. We were close friends, I would say, for probably fifteen years.

He taught me how to look at the world outside a city. I grew up in the Bronx. I didn't know what a mushroom was. I didn't know how to build a fire. Jack Doobie changed that.


---

Jack Doobie was the closest thing Ralph ever had to a mentor — and it is notable that he did not find one until he was in his fifties. Ralph had no teachers who believed in him as a young man (the one who liked him still gave him a D). He had no professional network, no family tradition of learning. Everything he knew, he had taught himself from books. Jack offered something different: knowledge transmitted in person, in the field, by someone who took the time to point things out. When Ralph says Jack taught him how to look at the world outside a city, he is describing something more than mushroom identification. He is describing what it feels like to learn from someone who is generous with what they know.

---


## Seventeen Acres

I told my wife I wanted to be a farmer, even though I grew up in New York City and had no idea what farmers did. I loved gardening, and I thought farming might be gardening on a grand scale. So I shopped around. I went upstate New York, couldn't find anything that was reasonably nice. They were all reasonably priced, but not reasonably nice. And then somebody in Sherman told me that one of the land trusts was splitting up a big, beautiful piece of land — two hundred acres, making six or seven lots out of it. I bought the biggest lot — seventeen plus acres. And the farm is surrounded by land trust. We have a few hundred acres in the back that are land trust. On one side, another hundred acres of land trust. On the other side, a horse farm.

When I first saw the property, I loved it. It had a barn with plenty of storage. It had water. It had power. So we were able to come right in and start farming almost immediately. If we didn't have a barn, we'd have had to build one. And barns are expensive.

But the farm was totally overrun. It hadn't been farmed for five years. Weeds five and six feet high. You couldn't see the ground anywhere. You can't use a lawn mower on six-foot weeds. So I realized I needed a tractor, a brush hog, a tiller, a front loader to pick things up. I bought a miniature tractor — seventeen horsepower.

I had no idea how to use it. They dropped the tractor off in the middle of winter, snow on the ground. I had a catalog. So I'm reading the catalog: this is how you turn it on, this is low, this is high, this is up, this is down. And I'm practicing in the barn all winter. How to raise and lower the implements, how to turn on the brush hog, how to take it off, how to put the tiller on.

Then in spring, I said, okay. I'm going to try it. I get on the tractor, brush hog down low. It doesn't want to budge because the weeds are heavy as can be. So I raise it up a couple inches, and now I'm going back and forth. And before you know it, in an hour or two, everything is level. You can see the ground. Then I go back on the brush hog right down to the ground. Now I can see earth.

Then I put the tiller on. I'm going to do seven rows of raspberries. And there's an arrow on top of the hood of the tractor. I have no idea what that's for. So I start to cut a row with the tiller. And I look at the row — it looks like an S. Because I have no idea how you make a straight row. So the first few rows look like S's, and I said, there's got to be a way to figure this out. That arrow — you line it up with a distant point in the direction you're going. If you keep that arrow pointed at that point, you'll go in a straight line. So my first three rows look like S's and the next four rows look like arrows. It looked like a farmer did it, not a New York City hack.

The barn was eighty by forty feet, on two levels — about sixty-five hundred square feet. It was a working barn. It had cow stanchions, manure trenches, a hay barn upstairs. And a milk room — a separate room that had to be kept completely clean because that's milk that's going to be pasteurized. You had to pass inspection. Very strict on milk.

So we didn't have a house. We moved into the milk room. We'd come up on weekends in winter. I had a little potty toilet, a refrigerator, a little electric stove. We could make coffee, make breakfast. It was like camping, but indoors. I had an electric heater because the barns weren't heated. The cows just gave off enough heat to keep the water from freezing.

Then in summer, before the house was built, we set up a tent — eight by eleven, quite large, but all we had in it were our clothes and a double bed. And sometimes we'd be asleep, and people would be picking raspberries, and they'd open the zip of the tent and throw the money in.

We sold my other house about a year later and built a house about six or seven hundred feet from the barn. Four bedrooms, beautiful views from every window, full basement, full attic. A nice house for family.

The farm has an interesting history. There's a pond at the end of the property near the road. I have pictures of horses and men standing outside the pond with a giant saw — almost a six-foot saw — sawing blocks of ice out of the pond, three and four feet deep, five or six feet long. They'd hook up ropes and pulleys, and one horse would pull the block up onto a wagon. Then they'd drive about six or seven hundred feet to the ice house, and they'd pack it in hay — the whole house, top and bottom. That hay would keep the ice past summer. They used the ice to chill the milk. The whole area was dairy farms. Nobody was growing anything but corn to feed the cows.

Later, Verizon — it was called Cellular One in those days — sent their agent. He says, oh, I have a proposal. You're going to feel like you won the lottery. They wanted to put antennas on the silo. I said, let me think about it. I didn't really need the money. But the barn needed work — the sills were rotted, the wiring was ancient, dangerous. So I said, I'll sign the contract if you fix the barn.

They said no at first. Then a week later, they came back and said okay. They brought in ten-by-ten oak timbers, twenty feet long. They jacked up the barn, replaced the sills, rewired the whole building. Three months of work. And later, when we wanted a commercial kitchen, they moved all the wiring again at no charge. Nobody touches their wiring — they do it themselves. The silo now has T-Mobile, Verizon, and AT&T. And I have a barn that's up to code.


---


## The Farmer's Market Years

I started selling at the farmer's market around 1995. I did it for about ten years. What you do is make sure you have your best products. I sold raspberries, rhubarb, asparagus, and Northern Kiwis.

The Northern Kiwis — you'll never see them in a market. And if you do, they can't be good, because you can only pick them when they're dead ripe. If you pick them slightly underripe, they don't ripen and they don't taste very good. And when you pick them dead ripe, you have to cut the stem just like you do with a cherry. They only last two or three days, and then they go too ripe.

People loved having fresh rhubarb, fresh raspberries, fresh asparagus, fresh Northern Kiwis. I would sell out in an hour, hour and a half. I'd make maybe four hundred dollars. For a farmer, that was quite a bit, because you did it in a couple of hours.

Then when I'd come back to the farm, I'd open up for pick-your-own raspberries. Mainly, pick-your-own was only successful Saturdays and Sundays. If it rained, it was a big issue. Nobody would come and pick in the rain. You can't pick wet raspberries because they spoil right away. It was very difficult to even break even selling produce, but it was a fabulous experience to put the plants in the ground and then pick a final product. That was an amazing experience.

Now, the Northern Kiwis. I bought them from a nursery woman who was very knowledgeable. She said, if you plant these roots, in two or three years you'll have more kiwis than you could possibly eat. Great. So I put them in. But I didn't realize that once they're established, they grow thirty feet a year. You have to build an arbor, and then you have to prune them extensively because thirty feet — it would take over the country.

I had an arbor at my house for shade. I thought, I'll just take a few sticks of the kiwi, make a point, stick them in the ground, see what happens. In two years, it completely covered the arbor and was growing into the pool. The kiwis in the field were overrunning everything.

And here's the other thing: they're invasive. When the birds eat the kiwis — full of moisture and seeds and sweetness — they drop the seeds, and kiwi vines take over. You can go to places now where whole forests are overtaken. They choke out everything. Grow ten, fifteen, twenty feet high.

I met the nursery woman again later. She's no longer in the nursery business. She says to me, it was one of the worst mistakes she ever made. It's so invasive that she's advised all her clients: if you still have it, take it out.

I became friendly with a couple of other farmers. They'd ask my advice. Two young boys whose father was a farmer — they were growing hay, which is very labor-intensive. They wanted to do something else. So they'd talk to me about what to grow.

They wanted to do Christmas trees. I said, that's not a good idea. People think you put in a tree, five years later you sell it for a hundred dollars. But you have to water it, prune it, keep the deer away, keep the bugs away. Insane amount of work, and you never know if you're getting a payout. So they decided against that.

Then they wanted to do pick-your-own pumpkins. I said, there's not enough people in town to support it. They said, well, we're going to do it anyway. They grew pumpkins, bought pumpkins, layered up two acres. They even took people out in a horse-drawn wagon. Very homey, very nice. But they didn't make any money because there weren't enough people.

Then they came up with chopped hay for mulch. They showed me a bale. I said, I think it's the best product ever. You can spread it around like powder. You can mold it into any shape you want. And a bale sells for seventeen dollars, where a bale of mulch hay would be two or three dollars. The profit margin is gigantic. They had Agway as a customer. They were doing extremely well.

And then I had to give up the raspberries entirely. There was a bug that came in — a brucella fly, the most peculiar insect I'd ever seen. Most insects lay their eggs in ripe fruit. This one laid its eggs in unripe fruit. The fruit would ripen and taste fine. But if you put it on the table, the larvae would come crawling out. Pretty disgusting. Pretty hard to sell, actually.

I got in touch with the agricultural department. They had a listing of sprays you could use. But you had to spray with a different insecticide every week for ten weeks. I said, no. I can't do that. You're going to eat these fruits. You're going to have the spray residue of five different chemicals on them. I said, I'm not doing this anymore. So I plowed it under and put in blackberries instead. They don't seem to have the same problem.

My dairy farmer neighbor once came down and said, I don't understand my asparagus. His asparagus ferns were about six inches high. Mine were six feet. I said, well, where did you buy the roots? He said, at Agway. I said, that's the problem. They sit in the store and dry out. They never come back to life. You have to buy from a root grower. They're picked a day or two before, refrigerated, packed in wet moss, shipped overnight. If you're not going to plant them right away, you have to refrigerate them. Agway just keeps them in a bin at seventy degrees.

It never really made any money, farming. But the experience of doing it — the money was a secondary issue.

## The Winemaker

About twenty years ago — something like that — Cornell University got in touch with me. They have a big winemaking department, and they were calling small fruit farmers to help them get another product that might make some profit. I never heard of fruit wine before.

I said to the guy, wow. Wineries I've seen cost millions of dollars. And he said, just get yourself some glassware, a pump. You don't have to spend a lot of money.

So I needed a license. Getting the wine license was a good trick. It's a long, complicated license — about fifty pages — and you have to go through Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms to get it. I made it my winter project that every night I would do at least one page. At the end of the winter, I had the whole form filled out. Submitted it. Approved. I had a winery, and we started with five hundred dollars. That was our total cost.

The first batch was sweet rhubarb wine. I had to chop the rhubarb by hand — tremendously difficult, because I needed one-inch pieces and rhubarb is very tough to cut. So I made a cutter, like a paper cutter, where I could pull down a big handle and cut ten stalks at a time. Then I put the rhubarb in a big tub with sugar and water. That's all — no chemicals of any kind. When everything was ready, I added the yeast. You don't put the yeast in until everything's ready to go.

The yeast start to build over a day or two, and then you've got a container that's like a cauldron — bubbling like mad. It gets hot, which is not good, because you want to ferment at a cooler temperature to keep the aromatics. So I took a big plastic container, filled it halfway, froze it, and put it in. That actually lowered the temperature quite well.

The bottling was primitive. I had a little homemade winemaker's gizmo — the wine comes into a half-gallon container, you put a bottle under it, pull a plunger, and it fills one bottle at a time. Maybe two or three minutes per bottle. If you're doing ten cases — a hundred and twenty bottles — you're talking hours.

But I made my first vintage. The sweet rhubarb was okay — a dessert wine, not terrible, not great. Then I made a dry rhubarb, which I really liked. We were drinking it. That was pretty good. Then a raspberry wine and a blackberry wine and a cherry wine. Before you know it, we had six or seven different fruit wines.

Over time, I scaled up. I got a stainless steel container with a variable top, so you could put the cover right on the wine and pump it up like a bicycle tire — no air gets at the wine. The wine was much better after that. I got better pumps. I got a bottling line that does four bottles at a clip, and by the time you get the fourth bottle in, the first one's ready. A hundred, two hundred bottles an hour. We got filters for the bottling machine. We got refrigerated tanks where we can ferment at fifty-five degrees — perfect temperature.

Now we have about twenty stainless steel tanks. We can do a thousand bottles a day if we want to. We do about twelve hundred cases a year.

I introduced a sparkling cider by accident. It's made with no chemicals whatsoever. Literally two ingredients: apple juice and yeast. That's it. We ferment it to about seven percent alcohol, then bottle it in champagne bottles immediately. The bottle is capped, the fermentation continues inside, the carbon dioxide can't go anywhere, and you end up with a fizzy cider that tastes fabulously good. A hundred percent natural.

Then Eric came in. He said, I'm going to plant grapes. He put in about twenty-five rows — Marquette, Cayuga, Frontenac, and a new clone of Sauvignon Blanc. Two whites, two reds. Grapes are the hardest thing to grow. I could grow anything — corn, hay, blackberries, raspberries, apples, peaches. But grapes have to be fertilized, weeded, treated for fungus, treated for insects. It's a constant battle. It's either too hot or too cold. On and on.

The typical Connecticut wine grapes make a wine that — in my taste — is undrinkable. But Eric bought really good varieties. He's making a very good white and a very good red.

He is in love with it. He can't get enough. He's out there almost all day and all night. I remember a few years ago, he came in from pruning — sun-up to sundown. He's exhausted. And I say to him, you're lucky we're not charging you for this therapy.

My wife didn't really want to play a role in the winery at first. But then she retired, and she loved the whole idea of being a farmer's wife. She got involved in the plantings, the decorating of the winery, how we were going to do wine tastings. She became a major supporter of it all.

And about fifteen years ago, Eric said he wanted to run the farm. I said, okay. It's yours. We worked together for about ten years. The last few years, I've really slowed down. I gave up the chainsaw last year. This year will probably be my last year on the tractor. It's getting a little more difficult for me to do physical work. I can still prune a little, mulch a little, weed a little. But the heavy lifting is Eric's now.

My grandson Max, Peter's son, also loves working on the farm. He knows the entire process — harvesting, processing, fermentation, bottling. He's in law school now, but he could be a lawyer and still be a winemaker. I was a teacher and still made wine. It's possible to do two things at the same time.


---

The farm, the farmer's market, the winery — Ralph describes them with the same practical directness he brings to everything. He does not sentimentalize the land. He does not speak of it in the pastoral language of someone who retreated to the country for spiritual renewal. He went to Connecticut because he wanted to grow things, and he stayed because the work suited him. The satisfaction was concrete: you put a plant in the ground, you tend it, you pick the fruit. For a man who had spent years troubleshooting semiconductor failures — problems invisible to the naked eye, measured in picoamps — there was something deeply satisfying about work you could see and taste.

But the farm also became something Ralph may not have intended: a gathering place. His daughter got married there. His grandchildren spent summers there. His friends visited. His son Eric, who had been a management information systems professional in the corporate world, fell in love with the land and the grapes and never left. The seventeen acres that Ralph bought from a land trust became, over forty years, the center of a family — the opposite of the two-bedroom apartment on 167th Street where five children grew up trying to escape.

# Part VII: What Remains

## Editorial Frame


Anita died on July 6, 2006 — in July, the month they had met, the month that held everything. She was the girl Ralph had seen on a beach when they were fifteen, the one who told her friend, "I'm going to marry that guy." They had been together for nearly sixty years. He was eighty when she died.

When you have lived long enough, the people who knew you when you were young begin to disappear. Ralph's brother and three sisters are gone. His best friend Herbie is gone. Most of the old group from the neighborhood is gone. Anita is gone. His second partner, Mary Anne, developed dementia about two years ago and moved to a memory care facility. The world Ralph built — the one that started on a blanket at Orchard Beach and expanded to include a career, a family, a farm, a winery, and friendships that spanned ninety years — has narrowed now to the things that remain: the land, the cat, the children and grandchildren who visit, and the memories that fill the rooms of a farmhouse where one man lives alone.

Ralph is ninety-two. He still lives on the farm, alone now except for a cat. He exercises every morning. He reads. He spends an hour or two in the garden when his body allows it. He can no longer lift heavy things. He gave up the chainsaw last year. This will probably be his last year on the tractor. But he is still here, on the land he cleared himself, in the house he built, surrounded by the evidence of a life that was never supposed to amount to much.

His granddaughter is getting married this summer, on the same spot where his daughter married decades ago. Same view, same land, different generation.

## Jax

After Anita died — almost twenty years ago, July sixth, 2006 — I eventually found a new partner, Mary Anne. She wanted a cat. So we were together a few years, and she went to a rescue place. They showed us some cats. And then they said, well, we have a cat that we think is perfect for you, but he's a feral cat. He can't be inside. He can never come inside. He's got his claws and his teeth for protection. He's lived in a barn, so he's not very social. And you have to be very careful about picking him up because if he jumps and puts his claws in there, you'll be in trouble.

He loved Mary Anne. It was her cat. She babied this cat, brushed him every day. He fell in love with her. But he's an outdoor cat, so we had to make arrangements for him to go in and out of the house as he chooses. We put a cat door with a passageway in the basement. We leave the doors up to the house open so he can come and go as he pleases. And he likes to be outside hunting a lot of the time.

Mary Anne, about two years ago, suffered from dementia and eventually had to go to memory care.

So I now have the cat. And the cat does not leave me alone for one minute. He's on top of me all the time. When I'm eating, he's on the table. If I go into another room, he follows me. If I go outside, he goes out. If I go inside, he comes in. He's always by my side. So I'm in love with the cat now.

His name is Jax — j-a-x. And it's been a change for me because I'm in charge. I have to feed him. I have to make sure his litter box is clean. He's a very fastidious cat — always cleaning himself, always grooming. It's like having a little child now that I have to make sure I can take care of. If I go away for a day or two, I have to make sure somebody comes in and gets him fresh water — he likes fresh water every day — and fresh food every day. And the litter box has to be clean because he won't use a dirty one. He's very particular about that.

I exercise every day. I do a little routine in the house — a combination of yoga, Pilates, and Tai Chi. Ten or fifteen minutes every morning. Then I go to three classes a week: stretch, yoga, Tai Chi. We have a swimming pool, but I can't swim anymore because I had both my knees replaced. For some reason, my knees sink. I looked up the density of titanium, and it's much heavier than bone, so I understand why they sink. But I seem to be the only one who has that problem.

I read novels — mostly Philip Roth, Saul Bellow, books that discuss the meaning and purpose of life. That's what I like to read at ninety-two. I'm not big on mysteries or romances. And Jax is there through all of it. On my lap when I'm reading. On the table when I'm eating. Following me room to room. I never thought I'd be a cat person, but here I am.

So now I have a responsibility.

My granddaughter is getting married this summer. Right down below the barn, in the same spot where my daughter got married decades ago. Same view, same land, different generation. It's a big wedding right down below, and a déjà vu.
