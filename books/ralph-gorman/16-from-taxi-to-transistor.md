type: memoir
title: "From Taxi to Transistor"
---

I never thought about going to school until I was literally desperate. I had run out of blue-collar jobs. I was driving a taxi, driving a truck, working in various factories, putting round pegs in round holes. It was mind-numbing. And so I decided at that point I had to change my life.

This was the Sputnik era, and scientists were in great demand. I needed a profession that would allow me to go to work and get a job immediately. I already had a baby on the way. If they would have said door catchers wanted, I would have been a door catcher. But they always advertised engineering, physics, chemistry. Those were the big fields.

On Friday — I remember this distinctly — I was driving a taxi. My wife was in her eighth month. She had just stopped work that weekend. They were calling me to pick up fares. And on Monday, I was wearing a suit, and I was an engineer. That was the transition. Friday, a cab. Monday, a suit.

I went to work for United Technologies in a research lab where they were making semiconductors. And at that point, semiconductors were in their infancy. Every step, no matter how trivial, an engineer would do. So I spent six months on the first step, six months on the second. I spent a year learning how to polish a crystal. One year till we got it perfect. Then a few months in this area, a few months in that area. And so I had an overall picture of every single step to make a semiconductor — from the growth of the crystal all the way to putting it in a package.

By the time I finished five years, everybody was a specialist. There was a specialist in the first step, a specialist in the second step, all the way to the very end. But there were very few people who had an overall view of the whole process. And that served me all during my consulting days. I could look at a chip and say, well, it's more likely the problem is in this area or that area.

I loved engineering because every day, there would be a new puzzle to solve. You'd get this assignment and work on it. It was like every day you have to work on this puzzle, make this thing work. I could spend three, four, five, six months on one puzzle. Then you finish that puzzle, they give you another. And there was a group of about eight of us — no one ever left to take another job because we all kind of fit in with each other. The supervisor was a super smart guy, and we all liked him.

The thing I didn't like was the uncertainty. Working for United Technology, they lose a helicopter contract in Tanganyika, and ten people have to be fired. It's hit or miss. When that happened, everybody started getting their résumés together. You don't know if you're the one until it happens. There were always two weeks where the rumors were flying. And it was almost every year. I managed to escape all of them, but I said, I don't want to live like this for my whole life.

I remember an engineer there who'd been there fifteen years. The following year, he was going to get his pension guaranteed — I don't remember what it's called. He was fired just before he was eligible. He was crying. And I said, I don't want to go through this again. So I left.

But I carried everything I'd learned there with me. That six years in the research lab, learning every step, gave me something most people didn't have. And later, when I became a consultant, it was the reason companies kept calling.
